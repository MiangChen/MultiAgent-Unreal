# General Collective Intelligence: Pushing Multi-Robot Systems into Open-World Applications

## Abstract

To push multi-robot systems into open-world applications for generating tangible value, it is essential to shift from specialized tasks to general tasks by developing **general collective intelligence**, thereby enabling them to intelligently handle multiple tasks, unforeseen situations, and natural interaction.

---

Research on multi-robot systems dates back to the 1980s [1]. After over 40 years of development, significant progress has been made in domains such as robotic manufacturing and drone light shows. Nevertheless, multi-robot systems remain largely confined to specialized tasks that can be accomplished by pre-programming. They still face considerable limitations when used in general tasks in open-world applications that may involve versatile goals, unforeseen situations, and flexible human-swarm interaction. Usability is a basic yet crucial metric for evaluating multi-robot systems. As candidly noted in [2], “no real-world application of swarm robotics exists,” which reflects the keen expectation within the research community for real-world applications of multi-robot systems. This article discusses the importance and potential approaches of empowering multi-robot systems to intelligently tackle general tasks.

As a researcher with over a decade of experience in multi-robot systems, I deeply relate to the challenges of translating these systems into open-world applications. My early work focused on theoretical research in cooperative control [3, 4]. Later, I shifted toward applying these theories to a practical multi-robot system: using micro aerial vehicles (MAVs) equipped with onboard vision to detect, localize, and capture target MAVs [5, 6]. After considerable effort, we finally realized this system [7]. However, frustration soon followed, as the system was cumbersome and difficult to use in practice. For example, frequent algorithm modifications were necessary to handle various corner cases, such as when the target moved out of the camera’s field of view or when the number of MAVs changed. More importantly, the system was limited to the target pursuit task and could not intelligently handle other related tasks, such as target search or patrol. Additionally, human-robot interaction remained inflexible, lacking support for natural language or other intuitive modalities. Although this reflects my personal experience, I believe it resonates with the experiences of many researchers in the field.

These experiences have led me to place **practical usability** at the core of multi-robot systems research. As noted by [2], “one should not forget that the long-term goal of swarm robotics research is to deploy in the real-world robot swarms that perform useful tasks.” I believe the field will only regain its vitality when multi-robot systems can be effectively applied in practice to produce tangible value. My perspective shares common ground with [2], but also differs, particularly in light of recent rapid advancements in artificial intelligence (AI) and robotics. Technically, I believe that the key to pushing multi-robot systems into practice lies in enhancing their **generality** – for instance, enabling them to support versatile goals, unforeseen situations, and flexible human-swarm interaction.

## Shift from specialization to generality

The shift from specialized tasks to general tasks is a core driver behind the recent explosive growth in AI. In fact, the very definition of “intelligence” involves generality: “Intelligence measures an agent’s ability to achieve goals in a wide range of environments” [8]. Given the prolonged focus of AI algorithms on specialized tasks, the term artificial general intelligence (AGI) was introduced to emphasize generality. Recently, the advancement of large language models (LLMs) marks a significant milestone toward AGI, demonstrating remarkable generality across a broad spectrum of tasks and hence bringing the prospect of AGI closer to reality [9, 10]. This shift from specialization toward generality is now extending to other domains [11], including humanoid robotics.

The history of humanoid robotics offers valuable insights for the future of multi-robot systems. Like multi-robot systems, humanoid robots have long been criticized for their inability to accomplish practical tasks. With a history spanning over 50 years, progress in body hardware, sensing and control, and cognition and planning has been slow, leading to a perception of limited applicability. However, recent years have witnessed an explosive growth in humanoid robotics, primarily thanks to the advancement of the generality of embodied intelligence. By leveraging their integrated mobility and manipulation capabilities, humanoid robots are now anticipated to handle a variety of tasks in households and industries without requiring per-task customization. It is this vision of generality that has allowed people to foresee the potential for practical application in the near future (though this remains debatable) and has consequently received extensive attention from both academia and industry.

In addition to the insights above, the development of complex and capable robots - such as humanoid, quadruped, and aerial robots [12] - offers important technical foundations for future multi-robot systems. Specifically, much prior research in multi-robot systems has relied on the assumption of simple, low-cost individual robots, expecting complex collective behaviors to emerge from simple local interactions. This paradigm was originally inspired by biological collectives such as ant colonies, fish schools, and bird flocks. However, it is important to note that biological individuals, while seemingly simple, are inherently complex: humans still struggle to replicate even partial capabilities of an ant, fish, or bird. The assumption of simple individual robots has arguably hindered the application of multi-robot systems to complex real-world applications. One important reason for adopting this assumption is that in the past we were unable to build robots as complex and capable as those achievable today.

The current rapid advancement of complex individual robots presents a new opportunity for multi-robot systems. I believe that future multi-robot systems will rely not on simple, low-cost robots, but on complex, capable, and heterogeneous ones. After many years of largely parallel development, the trajectories of multi- and single-robot systems are believed to intersect (**Fig. 1**). This intersection could propel multi-robot systems beyond their current plateau into a new phase of rapid growth. Then, an immediate question that follows is when and how this intersection will occur. I argue that the key lies in significantly enhancing the generality of multi-robot systems, enabling them to intelligently collaborate with humans on general tasks in open-world applications.

## General collective intelligence

To achieve generality, **general collective intelligence (GCI)** is a core future research direction that deserves more attention. The concept of GCI is broad and may describe sociological, biological, and even recent AI agentic collectives. Here, we focus specifically on GCI for multi-robot systems. Alternative terms such as general swarm intelligence (GSI) may also convey a similar notion, though I am nervous to adopt the term “swarm intelligence,” as it heavily emphasizes a bottom-up paradigm – from simple individuals to complex collectives – which is inconsistent with the GCI concept articulated here.

While a complete description of GCI is non-trivial, I argue that the GCI of multi-robot systems manifests in three dimensions. 

1.  **Multiple tasks:** While existing systems are often confined to specialized tasks such as cooperative pursuit, search, transport, or shape assembly, GCI should be able to handle a set of diverse tasks – this constitutes the very essence of generality. Of course, the boundary of the set of diverse tasks must be well defined. 
2.  **New situations:** Even a single task encompasses numerous potential situations due to variations in parameters such as the number of robots, their individual capabilities, and environmental conditions. GCI must be able to intelligently handle unforeseen situations, as pre-programming for all possible situations is unrealistic. 
3.  **Natural interaction:** GCI should support multi-modal human-robot interaction, with a particular emphasis on natural language. Human users should be able to flexibly instruct the system to specify or modify task goals, as well as to adjust the collaborative policies.

GCI can be intuitively regarded as a “collective brain.” A multi-robot system with GCI should integrate several levels of components: individual body for actuation and execution, individual cerebellum for sensing and control, individual brain for cognition and planning, and collective brain for cooperative task planning (**Fig. 2**). I believe a collective brain exhibits two characteristics.

**First, GCI prioritizes high-level task planning over low-level skills**, primarily due to two reasons. 
1) Generality and specialization are inherently conflicting: systems finely tuned to specialized robot dynamics or control struggle to be general. A hierarchical structure may offer greater potential to balance this trade-off, though the division of layers and their interfaces require careful design. 
2) The division of labor is inevitable in developing multi-robot systems nowadays: it is unrealistic to expect a single team to develop all components. Even a single humanoid robot may require multiple teams focusing on different subsystems such as body hardware, dexterous hands, and expressive faces, not to mention a highly complex multi-robot system. If the coordination of multiple robots is overly dependent on low-level robot dynamics, system integration would be difficult, not to mention incorporating new or heterogeneous types of robots. Therefore, GCI should emphasize high-level planning, ensuring flexibility and compatibility with diverse underlying individual robotic platforms. It should be noted, however, that hierarchical frameworks may not achieve policies as optimal as those produced by specialized algorithms [13]. From a fundamental perspective, hierarchy is adopted to trade some optimality for greater generality and flexibility.

> **Fig. 2:** A multi-robot system with GCI would integrate several levels of components: individual body for actuation and execution, individual cerebellum for sensing and control, individual brain for cognition and planning, and collective brain for cooperative task planning.

**Second, GCI combines centralized coordination with distributed execution.** Researchers may be interested in whether the collective brain is centralized or distributed. While distributed systems are typically praised for their robustness, my experience suggests a different perspective due to two reasons. 
1) Multi-robot systems are expected to follow human instructions in order to accomplish meaningful tasks. Such tasks usually involve global goals, which inevitably require some form of global or centralized coordination – even if the centralized coordination is implicit. For instance, shape assembly requires a group of robots to form a pre-specified shape, indeed a global goal. Although the resulting policies may appear distributed, their manual design is inherently centralized, and their implementation may also rely on pre-designed infrastructure [4]. 
2) A multi-robot system must always be monitored by human users, necessitating a central monitoring unit. This unit can naturally serve as a centralized coordinator without requiring an additional dedicated device. In many application scenarios, particularly in civil domains, the central unit is not as vulnerable as it is often perceived. Rather than overemphasizing concerns about its vulnerability, it is more important to focus on how to achieve generality. Researchers should not be afraid of adopting centralized coordination, as it is both natural and necessary for accomplishing meaningful tasks with global goals.

In terms of methodology, GCI should embrace emerging methods such as language models. The hierarchical separation between high-level task planning and low-level task execution is not new in robotics. However, traditional task planning methods rely primarily on model-based optimization, which tends to be specialized. A suitable integration of model-based optimization, data-driven learning, and language models is likely necessary. Model-based optimization offers strong reliability and interpretability but limited generality, whereas language models and learning methods provide strong generality with comparatively weaker reliability and interpretability. This complementarity is advantageous, indicating that practical systems will likely adopt hybrid methods. 

Beyond methodology, several implementation paradigms exist. One paradigm employs a single algorithmic framework capable of adaptively handling different tasks with minimal parameter tuning. For example, we recently proposed **GenSwarm**, an LLM-based framework that can generate, deploy, and execute multi-robot policies end-to-end from natural language commands for diverse multiple multi-robot tasks [14]. Another paradigm leverages the generalization capabilities of LLMs to pre-train policies that are deployable out-of-the-box for diverse tasks. An intermediate paradigm may use a foundation policy that performs well across domains, combined with online learning for continuous adaptation (**Fig. 3**).

## Value through applications

The perspectives in this article are derived from my theoretical and applied research in multi-robot systems over the past decade, incorporating my recent experiences with reinforcement learning and LLMs. I personally have become increasingly convinced of the critical importance of practical usability of multi-robot systems in general tasks in open-world applications. Only by enhancing their usability – enabling them to integrate into our daily lives, serve as reliable partners, and help solve real-world problems – can such systems create true value, attract greater attention, and thereby regain vitality. 

This article does not prescribe a specific technical pathway but aims to stimulate discussion. Some of the views presented here call for a **shift in research focus**: from low-level robot dynamics and control to higher-level task planning, from conventional model-based optimization to learning methods and language models, and from an emphasis on distributed coordination toward a balanced integration of centralized and distributed coordination. Greater emphasis should be placed on ultimate goals rooted in practical application, allowing methodological choices to be driven by these goals.
